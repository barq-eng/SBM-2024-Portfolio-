Work

Stirling Engine

Undergraduate course focused on manufacturing and redesigning a thermo-mechanical device (Stirling Engine seen above). We spent the first weeks of classes becoming familiar with our machinery handbook and various common notations within engineering drawings used for manufacturing. After being trained and shown how to use a mill, lathe, and waterjet cutter among other tools we were able to machine various parts to specific and tight tolerances.

Patience and attention to detail was crucial to the success of the project and the function of the engine. Due to the engine only working within tight thermal boundaries, the room for error for mechanical tolerances and misalignment was small. Quickly this became one of my favorite courses of my undergraduate career due to the balance between technical and hands on application of the material within the course.

This project aimed to enhance the efficiency and maneuverability of aerostructures, particularly wind turbine blades, by developing morphing trailing edge sections. Through extensive design, fabrication, and testing processes, the team explored multiple core designs using woven carbon fiber composites and Thermoplastic Polyurethane (TPU).

The final prototypes underwent rigorous testing, including finite element analysis and three-point bending tests, to validate their structural integrity and flexibility. The results demonstrated the potential of these morphing blades to significantly improve wind energy harvesting and reduce structural stress, paving the way for future advancements in aerostructure design. The photo above is of the final 3-D printed scaled down version of the trailing edge. Click on the photo for our poster and more information.

Undergraduate technical elective course on Finite Element Analysis where I gained hands-on experience in simulating 1D, 2D, and 3D structural and thermal systems using ANSYS. The course provided a comprehensive introduction to both the GUI and APDL interfaces within Ansys, allowing me to perform static and transient analyses with precision.

A key focus was on mastering the software and converting real life situations into simulations to be analyzed. Above is a screenshot of one of the many real life situations simulated throughout the course. In this case we were tasked with analyzing the thermal process of a "sprained" ankle (modeled in 2-D) submerged in an ice bath to determine the effectiveness of the treatment.
